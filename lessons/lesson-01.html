What Is a Computer Thinking? 🤖

⏱ 40 minutes 🧩 Decomposition 📊 Uni-structural
📋 NSW Outcomes: TE4-DIG-01 (technological literacy) · TE4-DIG-02 (code, design and produce) · Contributing: TE4-MSC-01

Learning Intentions

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  1. Explain what computational thinking means in your own words
  2. Identify the Input → Process → Output model in everyday technology
  3. Label the parts of a BBC micro:bit using correct names
  4. Write a log entry reflecting on your learning using the I learned / I tried / I wonder format
  5. Use the built-in simulator to test out code without needing a physical micro:bit
  6. Connect the micro:bit to a computer using the micro USB cable - if used

✅ Success Criteria

You'll know you've got it when you can:

  • ☐Name at least 3 inputs and 3 outputs on a micro:bit
  • ☐Draw or describe a real-world example using the IPO model
  • ☐Recognise the micro usb connector has a correct orientation and insert it properly
  • ☐Suggest a situation where the micro:bit might need a power source other than the USB cable
  • ☐Write first log entry using the I learned / I tried / I wonder format

📖 Key Vocabulary

Computational thinking
A way of solving problems by thinking like a computer — breaking them down into small, clear steps
Input
Information that goes INTO a device (e.g. pressing a button)
Processing
What the device DOES with that information (e.g. checks if the button was pressed)
Output
What comes OUT of the device (e.g. a light turns on)
Algorithm
A step-by-step set of instructions for solving a problem
micro:bit
A small, programmable computer the size of your hand
🎣THINK — What Does Your Brain Do?5 minutes

Think about this:

"When you catch a ball, what steps does your brain follow?"

Think about it, then share with a partner. You'll probably come up with something like:

  1. See the ball coming ← this is INPUT
  2. Calculate where it will land ← this is PROCESSING
  3. Move your hand to catch it ← this is OUTPUT
Congratulations — you just described computational thinking!
📡THEORY — The IPO Model10 minutes

Every computer — from your phone to a giant supercomputer — follows the same basic idea:

INPUTInformation coming IN
PROCESSINGWhat happens in the middle
OUTPUTWhat comes OUT

What is INPUT?

Input is any information that comes into a system. Examples on a micro:bit:

  • Pressing Button A or Button B
  • Tilting or shaking the micro:bit (accelerometer)
  • Temperature reading from the temperature sensor
  • Light level reading from the LED display
🔊 V2 only: Sound detected by the microphone

What is PROCESSING?

Processing is what happens in the middle. The computer follows your instructions (your code) to decide what to do.

Example: If Button A is pressed AND the temperature is above 30, turn on the display.

What is OUTPUT?

Output is what comes out of the system — how it responds. Examples on a micro:bit:

  • Lighting up the 5×5 LED display
  • Displaying a number or message
🔊 V2 only: Playing a sound through the speaker

The IPO Model in real life

DeviceInputProcessingOutput
Automatic doorMotion sensor detects youChecks if motion was detectedDoor opens
Traffic lightTimer counts downChecks if time is upChanges colour
Your phone alarmClock reaches set timeChecks if it matches alarm timeSound plays
🔬MEET YOUR MICRO:BIT10 minutes

Look at the micro:bit simulator. Just open python.microbit.org/v/3 and look at the simulator on the left). It's going to open a new window

Can you identify the parts?

Here is a diagram of the real micro:bit. What is different about the simulator?

Hint: The simulator is a digital representation of the micro:bit, so some features might be missing or behave differently. For example, the simulator doesn't have a physical compass sensor, so it won't show compass readings. Also, the simulator's buttons are clicked with a mouse instead of being physically pressed.

Inputs on the micro:bit

  • Button A (left button)
  • Button B (right button)
  • Accelerometer (detects movement, tilt, shake)
  • Temperature sensor
  • Light sensor (uses the LED display)
  • Compass (magnetometer)
🔊 V2 only: Microphone

Outputs on the micro:bit

  • 25 LEDs on the display (5 rows × 5 columns)
  • Pins on the bottom edge (can connect buzzers, motors, etc.)
🔊 V2 only: Built-in speaker

Task 2: Design an IPO

Choose ONE real-world device (not a micro:bit). Draw or write:

  • What is its input?
  • What does it process?
  • What is its output?

Examples: a toaster, a smoke alarm, a calculator, a lift/elevator, a hand dryer.

Extension Task

Open python.microbit.org/v/3. Look at the example code that loads when you first open the editor. Can you spot:
  • What is the input?
  • What is the output?
  • What do you think the processing does?
Write your answers in your log.
